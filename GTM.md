The strongest target user is likely an engineering manager or technical founder at a seed-to-Series-A startup with 5–40 employees, where AI tooling spend has grown organically without centralized ownership. They are the person most likely to feel the pain of overlapping seats, duplicated assistants, and quietly rising API bills, but they still have enough control to change the stack quickly. The product should meet them in places where they are already thinking about engineering tooling costs: indie hacker communities, founder Slack groups, dev-tool subreddits, and X discussions around Cursor, Copilot, Claude, and ChatGPT team plans.

The first 100 users would likely come from direct founder outreach tied to live examples, not broad content marketing. The credible zero-budget play is to post teardown-style spend audits, offer a free personalized review for startups sharing their current stack, and seed the tool into conversations where teams are already debating whether they need premium AI seats for everyone. Credex’s unfair advantage is that it can close the loop from “you are overspending” to “here is an operationally simple way to spend less” through discounted credits. That means the tool is not only a calculator; it is a lead qualifier for a real supply-side advantage.
